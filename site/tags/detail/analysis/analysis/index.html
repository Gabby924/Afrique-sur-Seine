01:44

These scenes of children originate from 1950 in the famously censored anti colonialist film Afrique 50 made by French filmmaker Rene Vautier. The use of this footage may act as a critique of Vautiers film, or quite possibly- a reconstruction of his idea. Shot with the original intention of relating African childhood to that of a universal sense of innocence and humanity, the scenes depiction of African people also lacks interiority. Though there is a feeling of shared personhood between the african image and French audiences in the original, there is no acknowledgment of the unique perspective of the African peoples. Through means as simple as pronoun usage, Vieyra assumes the use of “We” to clue the audience into an african perspective. (Close up: Paulin S. Vieyra, A Postcolonial Figure)

02:34

Two young African children walk through a corn field- away from the camera as the narrator speaks of African childhood in a past tense, as a metaphor for African civilization’s pupate state. Suggesting that growth comes in “-grow(ing) up, leave(ing) home for Paris.” This contextualizes the past few scenes of the children playing and the narrator professing the profound childish freedom of an ignorant African people, unaware of the world around them, as the precursor to growth in a larger african mindset- not to remain and grow within Africa, but to go elsewhere. Specifically, the west- France.

02:43

The narrator refers to Paris first in vague comparison to Africa- "sun gives way to mist, machine noises to men of nature”. In documentaries of the expository mode, an omniscient voice of god-like narration serves to decide what each image shown is meant to communicate- leaving no room for poetic interpretation. Mostly used as propaganda film, these films display film truth as absolute. The narration aspect of Afrique Sur Seine and it’s poetic, but, quite obviously nationalist imagery: “Paris is also the city of promises made by young men to help their country” serves what purpose? To present Paris as it is, objectively yet sensuously? That could be done with a few words of the flora, crowded streets, traffic sounds and history. As meditation on the cultural consciousness of Parisian culture to foreigners? There’s far more intention than for that sole purpose. As the narrator says himself at the 02:52 mark, “But Paris is the centre of hope, all hope.” Afrique Sur seine may be utilizing this propagandizing power to call African people to Paris, to the only place where upward mobility-or growth- is possible.

03:25

Following the Narrator’s question to a personified Paris, “Where are your gold-paved streets from our nursery books?” A man’s legs, in clean black dress shoes and dark trouser pant legs, jog down a cobble stone street. If this cobblestone street is representative of one paved in gold, the gold in this circumstance is a metaphorical wealth. The camera tilts up revealing the man to be black, presumably African- following after a white woman as she hops onto a trolley and off and away into the city- surrounded by white people. So, following the logic of narration, image and theme- if a man, following a golden path- out from childishness and ignorance toward globalism and education what does the trolley’s symbolism serve? Well, public transportation in film is often used to represent a characters feelings of liminality and existential social immersion. If a character is in a large bus filled with people, it’s worth asking: Who are these people, and from what culture? The same as our protagonist, or a foreign one? Where is the bus going? What is it passing through? Where did it come from? And how does our character carry themselves? Proudly and exuberantly, interacting with the people around them? Silent and reserved, showing their feelings of isolation and disconnect? In this case, a black man in a European suit jumps onto the trolley with energy and lightness, surrounded by white people (likely parisians) he does not look to be rejected by the other passengers or dejected in any sense. Instead he seems to be the same as any other. What does this representation, illusory or not, serve to the African audience as propaganda? It presumably says, this foreign land is opportune and intimidating, as displayed in the long sweeping shot just preceding the trolley scene which shows the magnitude of Paris- beautiful but undeniably big. But, this land also allows African peoples an opportunity for normalcy, without subjugation. A fear that might otherwise dissuade potential immigrants.

03:45

Calling back to these streets, the african man from the trolley hops off onto the street and greets foreign friends outside a restaurant. With a confidant gait, and a face of content- the man continues his walk through these streets “paved with gold”. These same streets the narrator claims to be the streets of Boulevard Saint-Germain, Rue de l’Odeon, La Maison Moliere; Parisian streets that exist beyond the nameless paradise described in nursery rhymes. Every step the African man takes on these streets, in this city, cement his validity as an inhabitant.

15:36

The theme of interracial relationships, sexual or not, reoccur in Afrique-sur-seine. As Brett Bowles points out, scholars of african cinema have long examined Afrique-sur-seine through it’s portrayal of Black African peoples in Paris- here we see the assimilation of it’s peoples as deeply cultural and sociological. Vieyra goes so far as to show African and Parisians having intimate relationships at a time where black and white people touching or interacting on screen as equals was considered shocking.

16:30

Positive language is noticeably employed in the film, rarely is there a negative or reductive phrase. Vieyra’s intention in positive, additive verbiage, is possibly meant to further enhance the discussion of immigration and heterogeneous society as positive, in that; nothing I being taken away from French or African people, but added to French and African culture.

16:48

The choice for the subjects to blend into passerby, for the filmmaker to make no discernible effort to accentuate or draw attention to them lends to Vieyra’s themes of assimilation as a visual motif.

Analysis

17:15

Food and acts of communion contain a lot of material for analysis, especially in this scene. A mixed group of people, all around the same age, sit at a table talking and eating. At a closer glance we see that they may be sharing African food. Viewers will first register this meal as a gathering of friends, seeing Vieyra’s intention in showing mixed groups in social exchange. Through the use of an African meal as the vehicle of connection, Vieyra presents a much more intense cultural exchange.

Analysis

19:17

Shadowed faces, purposefully walking away from any source of light which might identify them, pull the viewer in to an added awareness of their status as an observer. The subjectivity and bias that comes with being observer are presented as well. The men are faceless, raceless, and yet the use of African music in the background compels the viewer to assume the men are black.

21:12

The use of this music, carrying into the final shot of the scene and into the credits suggests the tone of what Vieyra hopes for the audience to carry after the film’s end. I would argue that it’s the continued sense of assimilation. The music plays even as the film ends, embedding itself in the mind of the viewer.
